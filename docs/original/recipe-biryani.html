Chicken Biryani

Cuisine: Indian Prep: 30 min Cook: 45 min Marination: 2-4 hours Total: 75 min active Servings: 6 Difficulty: Medium Rating: 4.9 / 5 (3,456 reviews)

Biryani is the undisputed king of Indian rice dishes — a Mughal-era masterpiece that has been perfected over centuries across the subcontinent. This Hyderabadi-style kacchi biryani layers raw marinated chicken with par-cooked basmati rice, then slow-cooks them together under a sealed lid (dum), allowing the spiced meat juices to steam upward through the rice. The result is extraordinary: each grain of rice is separate and infused with saffron and spice, while the chicken at the bottom becomes fall-off-the-bone tender. The golden fried onions, fresh mint, and saffron milk create layers of flavor that no quick-cook method can replicate.

Ingredients

  • Chicken marinade:
  • 800g bone-in chicken pieces (thighs and drumsticks)
  • 200g thick yogurt (full-fat Greek yogurt)
  • 2 tbsp ginger-garlic paste
  • 2 tsp Kashmiri red chili powder
  • 1 tsp turmeric powder
  • 1 tsp garam masala
  • 1 tbsp lemon juice
  • 1.5 tsp salt
  • 2 tbsp fresh mint leaves, chopped
  • 2 tbsp fresh cilantro, chopped
  • Rice:
  • 500g aged basmati rice (soaked in cold water for 30 minutes)
  • 3 liters water for boiling
  • 4 green cardamom pods
  • 4 whole cloves
  • 1 bay leaf
  • 1 cinnamon stick (5cm)
  • 1 star anise
  • 2 tsp salt
  • Layering and dum:
  • Large pinch of saffron threads (about 25 strands), soaked in 3 tbsp warm milk
  • 3 large onions, thinly sliced and deep-fried until dark golden (birista)
  • 3 tbsp ghee, melted
  • 2 tbsp fresh mint leaves
  • 2 tbsp fresh cilantro leaves
  • 2 green chiles, slit lengthwise
  • Dough for sealing: 1 cup flour + water (or foil and a heavy lid)

Step-by-Step Instructions

  1. Marinate the chicken: Combine chicken pieces with yogurt, ginger-garlic paste, chili powder, turmeric, garam masala, lemon juice, salt, mint, and cilantro. Mix thoroughly, ensuring every piece is coated. Cover and refrigerate for at least 2 hours, preferably 4 hours or overnight. The yogurt tenderizes the meat while the spices penetrate deeply.
  2. Fry the onions (birista): Heat 250ml vegetable oil in a deep pan over medium-high heat. Fry thinly sliced onions in batches, stirring frequently, for 12-15 minutes until deep golden brown and crispy. Remove with a slotted spoon and drain on paper towels. They will continue to darken as they cool. Reserve the onion-infused oil for cooking. These fried onions are the secret weapon of great biryani — they add sweetness, crunch, and an incredible depth of flavor.
  3. Par-cook the rice: Bring 3 liters of water to a rolling boil with cardamom, cloves, bay leaf, cinnamon, star anise, and salt. Drain the soaked rice and add to the boiling water. Cook for exactly 5-6 minutes until the rice is 70% cooked — the grain should still have a firm white core when you bite it. Drain immediately and spread on a tray to stop cooking.
  4. Layer the biryani: In a heavy-bottomed pot or Dutch oven, spread the marinated chicken in an even layer at the bottom. Scatter half the fried onions, half the mint, and half the cilantro over the chicken. Add the slit green chiles. Spread the par-cooked rice evenly on top. Drizzle the saffron milk in streaks across the surface (do not mix). Top with the remaining fried onions, mint, and cilantro. Drizzle melted ghee over everything.
  5. Seal and cook on dum: Seal the pot tightly. Traditional method: make a stiff dough with flour and water, roll into a rope, and press around the rim before placing the lid. Shortcut: cover with a double layer of aluminum foil, then press the lid on firmly. Place over high heat for 3-4 minutes until you hear it sizzling, then reduce to the lowest possible heat and cook for 35-40 minutes. Do not open the lid during cooking — the trapped steam is doing the work.
  6. Rest and reveal: Remove from heat and let rest, still sealed, for 5 minutes. Break the seal, remove the lid, and gently fold the layers together using a wide, flat spatula, being careful not to break the rice grains. The bottom layer (tah-dig) should have a golden, slightly crispy crust — this is the most prized portion.
  7. Serve: Mound the biryani on a large serving platter. Garnish with extra fried onions, fresh mint, and toasted cashews or almonds if desired. Serve with raita (yogurt with cucumber and mint), mirchi ka salan (green chili curry), and a simple kachumber salad of diced onions, tomatoes, and cucumber with lemon juice.

Chef Tips

  • Use aged basmati rice: Aged basmati (1-2 years old) absorbs less water and produces longer, more separate grains. Fresh basmati tends to become sticky and clump. Look for "aged" or "extra-long grain" on the packaging. Indian grocery stores carry the best varieties.
  • The 70% rule for rice: Par-cooking the rice to exactly 70% doneness is the most critical technical step. Undercooked rice will remain hard; overcooked rice will turn mushy during the dum phase. The grain should bend without snapping and have a visible white core.
  • Do not skip the fried onions: Birista is not a garnish — it is a fundamental flavoring agent. The caramelized sugars in fried onions provide the signature sweetness and complexity that defines great biryani. Use at least 3 large onions; they reduce dramatically in volume.
  • Seal the pot properly: The dum cooking method relies on trapped steam. Any gap in the seal allows steam to escape, resulting in dry rice and undercooked chicken. The flour dough seal is traditional and most effective. A tight foil seal is an acceptable shortcut.
  • Bone-in chicken only: Boneless chicken dries out during the long dum cooking. Bone-in thighs and drumsticks stay succulent and release collagen that enriches the rice from below. This is one dish where bones are essential, not optional.
