Massaman Curry

Cuisine: Thai Total: 2 hours Difficulty: Medium Servings: 6 Rating: 4.9 / 5

Massaman Curry is a rich, mildly spiced Thai curry with Persian and Indian roots, reflecting centuries of trade between Thailand and the Muslim world. Unlike fiery Thai curries, Massaman is warm and aromatic, built on a foundation of cinnamon, cardamom, cloves, star anise, and cumin, braised low and slow with tender beef, waxy potatoes, and roasted peanuts in luscious coconut milk. It has been called the king of curries and was once ranked the world's most delicious food.

Ingredients

  • 800g beef chuck, cut into 4cm cubes
  • 2 cans (800ml total) full-fat coconut milk
  • 4 tbsp Massaman curry paste
  • 3 medium waxy potatoes, peeled and quartered
  • 1 large onion, cut into wedges
  • 1/2 cup roasted peanuts
  • 3 tbsp fish sauce
  • 3 tbsp palm sugar (or brown sugar)
  • 2 tbsp tamarind paste
  • 3 cardamom pods, lightly crushed
  • 1 cinnamon stick
  • 2 star anise
  • 3 bay leaves
  • 1 cup water or beef stock
  • Thai basil leaves for garnish
  • Steamed jasmine rice for serving

Step-by-Step Instructions

  1. Crack the coconut cream: Open the cans of coconut milk without shaking. Scoop the thick cream from the top (about 1 cup) into a large heavy-bottomed pot or Dutch oven. Heat over medium-high heat until the cream breaks and oil begins to separate on the surface, about 5 minutes.
  2. Fry the curry paste: Add the Massaman curry paste to the cracked coconut cream. Stir and fry for 3-4 minutes until the paste is deeply fragrant, darkened in color, and the oil pools around the edges. This step is critical for developing the curry's depth of flavor.
  3. Brown the beef: Add the beef cubes to the pot and stir to coat thoroughly in the paste. Sear for 3-4 minutes, turning occasionally, until the beef is browned on the outside and well coated with the aromatic paste.
  4. Build the curry: Pour in the remaining coconut milk and water or stock. Add the cinnamon stick, cardamom pods, star anise, and bay leaves. Stir in the fish sauce, palm sugar, and tamarind paste. Bring to a gentle boil, then reduce heat to the lowest setting.
  5. Braise: Cover and simmer very gently for 1 hour, stirring occasionally. The curry should barely bubble. After 1 hour, add the potato quarters and onion wedges. Continue simmering, covered, for another 30-40 minutes until the potatoes are tender and the beef is fork-tender and falling apart.
  6. Finish the curry: Stir in the roasted peanuts. Taste and adjust the balance of flavors: add more fish sauce for salt, palm sugar for sweetness, or tamarind for tang. The curry should be rich, slightly sweet, mildly sour, and deeply savory. Remove the whole spices if desired.
  7. Serve: Ladle the curry over steamed jasmine rice. Garnish with Thai basil leaves and a scattering of extra roasted peanuts. Serve with a wedge of lime on the side.

Chef Tips

  • Crack the coconut cream: Frying the curry paste in cracked coconut cream (where the oil separates) is a fundamental Thai technique. It blooms the spices in the paste and builds an aromatic foundation that simply dissolving paste in liquid cannot achieve.
  • Low and slow braising: Massaman needs time. The long, gentle simmer breaks down the beef's connective tissue into gelatin, creating that meltingly tender texture. Do not rush this by increasing the heat.
  • Balance the four flavors: Thai cooking is about balance: sweet (palm sugar), salty (fish sauce), sour (tamarind), and spicy (curry paste). Taste the final curry and adjust each element until it sings in harmony.
  • Waxy potatoes: Use waxy potatoes (like Yukon Gold) that hold their shape during the long braise. Starchy potatoes like Russets will disintegrate and muddy the curry.
  • Better the next day: Like many braises, Massaman curry improves dramatically after resting overnight in the refrigerator. The flavors deepen and meld. Reheat gently and adjust seasoning before serving.
