Thai Green Curry

Cuisine: Thai Prep: 15 min Cook: 20 min Total: 35 min Servings: 4 Difficulty: Easy Rating: 4.8 / 5 (2,678 reviews)

Gaeng Khiao Wan — Thai green curry — is the most aromatic and herbaceous of all Thai curries. Its vivid green color comes from a paste of fresh green chiles, cilantro roots, lemongrass, galangal, and makrut lime leaves pounded in a mortar. Combined with rich coconut milk, Thai basil, and tender chicken, it delivers a symphony of flavors: spicy, sweet, salty, and herbaceous all at once. While the paste can be made from scratch, a quality store-bought paste makes this an achievable weeknight dinner that tastes far better than any takeout. Serve it over steaming jasmine rice and let the fragrance fill your kitchen.

Ingredients

Showing quantities for 4 servings

  • 500g boneless, skinless chicken thighs, sliced 1cm thick
  • 400ml coconut milk (full-fat, do not shake the can)
  • 200ml coconut cream (or the thick cream from the top of an unshaken coconut milk can)
  • 3-4 tbsp Thai green curry paste (Mae Ploy or Maesri brands, or homemade)
  • 200g Thai eggplant, quartered (or regular eggplant, cubed 2cm)
  • 150g bamboo shoots (canned, drained and rinsed)
  • 100g green beans, cut into 4cm lengths
  • 1 red bell pepper, sliced into thin strips
  • 4 makrut (kaffir) lime leaves, torn with center vein removed
  • 1 cup Thai basil leaves (holy basil if available)
  • 2 tbsp fish sauce (nam pla), plus more to taste
  • 1 tbsp palm sugar or light brown sugar
  • 2 large red chiles, sliced diagonally (for garnish and heat)
  • 1 tbsp vegetable or coconut oil
  • Steamed jasmine rice for serving

Step-by-Step Instructions

  1. Crack the coconut cream: Scoop the thick coconut cream from the top of the unshaken can into a wok or large saucepan over medium-high heat. Cook, stirring occasionally, for 3-4 minutes until the cream "cracks" — the oil separates and you see oily bubbles on the surface. This process concentrates the fat and creates the base for blooming the curry paste.
  2. Bloom the curry paste: Add the green curry paste to the cracked coconut cream. Stir-fry vigorously for 2 minutes until incredibly fragrant and the paste darkens slightly. The oil should take on a green tint and you should be able to smell the lemongrass, galangal, and chiles distinctly. This step is essential — it activates the aromatics and removes the raw taste from the paste.
  3. Cook the chicken: Add the sliced chicken to the paste and stir-fry for 3 minutes until the pieces are coated in the green paste and the exterior turns white. The chicken will finish cooking in the sauce.
  4. Add coconut milk and vegetables: Pour in the remaining coconut milk and bring to a gentle simmer. Add eggplant and green beans first (they take longer to cook). Simmer for 5 minutes. Then add bamboo shoots, red bell pepper, and torn makrut lime leaves. Simmer for another 3-4 minutes until all vegetables are tender but still have some bite.
  5. Season the curry: Add fish sauce and palm sugar. Stir and taste — the curry should be a balance of salty (fish sauce), sweet (sugar), spicy (paste), and rich (coconut). Adjust to your preference. Thai curries are meant to be boldly seasoned, not subtle.
  6. Finish with herbs: Remove from heat. Stir in the Thai basil leaves — the residual heat will wilt them gently while preserving their flavor and color. Adding basil to actively boiling curry destroys its delicate aroma.
  7. Serve: Ladle into bowls over steamed jasmine rice. Garnish with sliced red chiles, extra Thai basil leaves, and a drizzle of coconut cream. For extra heat, serve with Thai bird's eye chiles on the side. The curry should be soupy enough to soak into the rice.

Chef Tips

  • Crack the coconut cream first: This technique, standard in Thai cooking, separates the coconut fat and uses it as a frying medium for the curry paste. Skipping this step and dumping everything together results in a less intense, muddier curry. The separated oil carries the aromatic compounds more effectively.
  • Quality curry paste matters: If not making paste from scratch, use a reputable Thai brand (Mae Ploy, Maesri, or Cock Brand). Western supermarket brands often contain fillers and lack the fresh herb intensity of authentic Thai paste. Asian grocery stores are your best source.
  • Do not boil the basil: Thai basil should be added off the heat or at the very end. Boiling destroys its volatile oils and turns the leaves black. A brief wilt in residual heat preserves both flavor and the beautiful green color.
  • Thai eggplant vs. regular: Small, round Thai eggplants (the green or white golf-ball-sized variety) have a slightly bitter, firm texture that is traditional. Regular globe eggplant works but is softer and milder. If using globe eggplant, add it later to avoid mushiness.
  • Adjust the heat level: Start with 3 tablespoons of paste for moderate heat. Thai green curry paste varies significantly between brands — taste the paste first before deciding how much to use. You can always add more, but you cannot take it away. For milder curry, use more coconut milk to dilute the heat.
