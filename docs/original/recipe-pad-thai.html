Pad Thai

Cuisine: Thai Prep: 15 min Cook: 15 min Total: 30 min Servings: 4 Difficulty: Easy Rating: 4.9 / 5 (4,128 reviews)

Pad Thai is Thailand's most famous noodle dish, but its history is surprisingly political. In the 1930s and 1940s, Prime Minister Plaek Phibunsongkhram promoted Pad Thai as part of a national identity campaign, encouraging Thai citizens to eat noodles made from domestic rice rather than imported Chinese wheat. The dish was designed to be distinctly Thai, combining the country's signature flavor pillars — sour tamarind, salty fish sauce, sweet palm sugar, and spicy dried chiles — into a single, harmonious stir-fry. Cooked in a screaming-hot wok, the flat rice noodles absorb the complex sauce while developing a slight char, then are tossed with plump shrimp, scrambled egg, crunchy bean sprouts, and toasted peanuts. A squeeze of fresh lime and a scattering of chive blossoms complete this masterpiece of balance. The secret to great Pad Thai at home is cooking in small batches with maximum heat and having every ingredient prepped and within arm's reach before the wok hits the flame.

Ingredients

  • 200g dried flat rice noodles, 3-5mm width (Chantaboon brand recommended)
  • 300g large shrimp (16-20 count), peeled and deveined, tails removed
  • 3 tbsp tamarind paste concentrate (seedless, Cock brand or Mae Ploy)
  • 3 tbsp fish sauce (Squid brand or Megachef premium)
  • 2 tbsp palm sugar or light brown sugar, finely chopped
  • 1 tbsp oyster sauce
  • 3 large eggs, lightly beaten
  • 4 cloves garlic, minced
  • 1/4 cup dried shrimp, soaked in warm water for 10 minutes, then drained and roughly chopped
  • 3 tbsp roasted unsalted peanuts, roughly crushed, plus extra for garnish
  • 200g fresh bean sprouts (mung bean)
  • 4 stalks garlic chives (or green onions), cut into 5cm lengths, plus lime wedges, dried red chile flakes, and fresh cilantro for serving

Step-by-Step Instructions

  1. Soak the noodles: Place the dried rice noodles in a large bowl and cover with room-temperature water (not hot). Soak for 25-30 minutes until the noodles are pliable and white but still firm to the bite — they should bend easily but feel slightly chewy when you bite one. Do not use hot water or over-soak, as the noodles will become mushy and fall apart during stir-frying. Drain thoroughly in a colander and set aside. The noodles will finish cooking in the wok with the sauce.
  2. Make the Pad Thai sauce: In a small bowl, combine the tamarind paste, fish sauce, palm sugar, and oyster sauce. Stir until the sugar dissolves completely. Taste the sauce — it should have a balanced sweet-sour-salty profile with the tamarind tang being the dominant note, rounded by sweetness and sharpened by the fish sauce. Adjust to your preference: more sugar for sweeter, more tamarind for tangier, more fish sauce for saltier. Set aside. This sauce is the heart of the dish, so getting the balance right at this stage is critical.
  3. Prepare for high-heat cooking: Pad Thai must be cooked over the highest heat possible, and it comes together in under 5 minutes, so mise en place is essential. Arrange all your prepped ingredients in order of use near the stove: oil, garlic, shrimp, dried shrimp, eggs, noodles, sauce, bean sprouts, chives, and peanuts. Have a wok spatula or two wooden spoons ready. If cooking for 4, work in two batches of 2 servings each — overcrowding the wok drops the temperature and steams the noodles instead of frying them.
  4. Stir-fry the shrimp: Heat a wok or large carbon-steel skillet over the highest heat until smoking. Add 2 tablespoons of neutral oil (peanut or vegetable) and swirl to coat. Add the minced garlic and stir-fry for 10 seconds until fragrant. Immediately add the shrimp in a single layer and cook without moving for 60-90 seconds until the bottoms turn pink and develop golden spots. Flip and cook for 30 seconds more — the shrimp should be just barely cooked through. Push to the side of the wok or transfer to a plate.
  5. Cook the eggs and noodles: Push the shrimp to one side and add a splash more oil to the empty side of the wok. Pour in the beaten eggs and let them set for 15-20 seconds, then scramble into large, soft curds. Add the drained noodles and the chopped dried shrimp to the wok. Pour the Pad Thai sauce directly over the noodles. Using tongs or chopsticks, toss and fold the noodles vigorously to coat them evenly with the sauce. Continue tossing over high heat for 2-3 minutes until the noodles are tender and have absorbed the sauce, developing a slight char on some edges.
  6. Add the finishing touches: Toss in the crushed peanuts, bean sprouts, and garlic chives. Stir-fry for just 30 seconds — the bean sprouts should retain their crunch, and the chives should wilt slightly but stay vibrant green. The textural contrast between the soft noodles, crunchy sprouts, and crumbly peanuts is essential to the dish's appeal. Remove the wok from heat.
  7. Plate with care: Transfer immediately to warm plates or a large serving platter. Arrange the shrimp prominently on top. Garnish with a generous handful of extra crushed peanuts, a mound of fresh bean sprouts on the side, a few sprigs of fresh cilantro, and two lime wedges per serving. Serve small dishes of dried red chile flakes, extra fish sauce, and sugar on the side — these are the traditional condiments that allow each person to fine-tune the flavor to their preference.
  8. Serve immediately: Pad Thai waits for no one. The noodles begin to stick and clump as they cool, and the bean sprouts lose their crunch within minutes. Squeeze the lime generously over the noodles before eating — the bright acidity lifts every other flavor in the dish. For an authentic Bangkok street-food experience, eat standing at the counter with a cold Singha beer or a glass of sweet Thai iced tea on the side.

Chef Tips

  • Tamarind paste quality: The quality of your tamarind paste makes or breaks this dish. Avoid tamarind "concentrate" that comes in a jar with added sugar — it is cloyingly sweet and one-dimensional. Instead, buy a block of seedless tamarind pulp (Cock brand is excellent), soak a golf-ball-sized piece in 1/4 cup warm water for 15 minutes, then press through a fine strainer to extract a thick, complex, deeply sour paste. This fresh paste has a fruitiness and depth that bottled products cannot replicate.
  • Wok hei is the goal: "Wok hei" — the breath of the wok — is the smoky, charred flavor that defines great stir-fried noodles. It only develops at extremely high temperatures (well above what most home stoves produce). Use a carbon steel wok if you have one, preheat it until smoking, and cook in small batches. A cast-iron skillet is the best alternative. Non-stick pans cannot achieve wok hei because they cannot be heated high enough.
  • Soak noodles in room-temperature water: This is counter-intuitive, but using room-temperature water gives you far more control than hot water. Hot water softens the outer layer too quickly while the core stays hard, leading to unevenly cooked noodles that break apart. Room-temperature soaking takes longer (25-30 minutes) but produces uniformly pliable noodles that finish cooking perfectly in the wok.
  • Cook in batches, never overcrowd: The single biggest mistake home cooks make with Pad Thai is cooking a full 4-serving recipe in one batch. The wok temperature drops below the threshold for stir-frying, and the noodles steam in their own moisture instead of frying. This produces soggy, gummy, lifeless Pad Thai. Cook 2 servings at a time maximum — the entire cooking process takes under 4 minutes per batch.
  • Dried shrimp are essential: Dried shrimp (kung haeng) provide an intense umami depth that anchors the entire dish. They are not optional. Soak briefly in warm water to soften, then chop roughly. Look for medium-sized, orange-pink dried shrimp at any Asian grocery store — they should smell pleasantly briny, not ammoniac. A tablespoon of chopped preserved radish (chai poh) can also be added for an even more authentic flavor.
