French Onion Soup

Cuisine: French Total: 75 min Difficulty: Medium Servings: 4 Rating: 4.8 / 5

French Onion Soup, or Soupe a l'Oignon Gratinee, is a Parisian bistro staple that transforms the humblest ingredient into an extraordinary dish. Slowly caramelized onions dissolve into a deeply savory beef broth, ladled into oven-safe crocks and crowned with a thick slice of toasted baguette blanketed in melted Gruyere cheese. Every spoonful delivers a perfect balance of sweet, savory, and rich.

Ingredients

  • 6 large yellow onions (about 1.5 kg), thinly sliced
  • 3 tbsp unsalted butter
  • 1 tbsp olive oil
  • 1 tsp sugar
  • 1 tsp salt
  • 1/2 cup dry white wine or dry sherry
  • 6 cups beef stock (homemade preferred)
  • 2 cups chicken stock
  • 2 sprigs fresh thyme
  • 1 bay leaf
  • 1 tbsp Worcestershire sauce
  • Freshly ground black pepper to taste
  • 4 thick slices of day-old baguette, toasted
  • 200g Gruyere cheese, grated
  • 2 tbsp Cognac (optional)

Step-by-Step Instructions

  1. Caramelize the onions: Melt butter with olive oil in a large heavy-bottomed pot or Dutch oven over medium heat. Add all the sliced onions, stir to coat, and cook for 10 minutes until softened and translucent. Reduce heat to medium-low, add sugar and salt, and continue cooking for 35-40 minutes, stirring every 5 minutes, until the onions are deeply golden brown and jammy.
  2. Deglaze: Increase heat to medium-high and pour in the white wine. Scrape up all the caramelized fond from the bottom of the pot with a wooden spoon. Cook until the wine has nearly evaporated, about 2-3 minutes. If using Cognac, add it now and let it cook off for 1 minute.
  3. Build the soup: Add beef stock, chicken stock, thyme sprigs, and bay leaf. Bring to a boil, then reduce heat and simmer uncovered for 20 minutes. Stir in the Worcestershire sauce and adjust seasoning with salt and pepper. Remove the thyme sprigs and bay leaf.
  4. Prepare the croutons: While the soup simmers, preheat your oven broiler to high. Toast the baguette slices until golden on both sides. Set aside.
  5. Assemble the gratinee: Ladle the hot soup into four oven-safe crocks or ramekins, filling them about three-quarters full. Place a toasted baguette slice on top of each. Pile a generous mound of grated Gruyere over the bread, letting it extend slightly over the edges of the crock.
  6. Broil: Place the crocks on a sheet pan and broil for 3-5 minutes, watching carefully, until the cheese is bubbling, melted, and deeply golden brown with charred spots.
  7. Serve: Let the crocks rest for 2-3 minutes (they are extremely hot). Serve on a plate with a warning and a napkin. Eat by breaking through the cheese crust with your spoon to reach the rich broth beneath.

Chef Tips

  • Patience with onions: True caramelization takes 35-45 minutes over medium-low heat. Do not rush this step by raising the heat. The slow Maillard reaction is what builds the deep, complex sweetness that defines this soup.
  • Onion variety: Yellow onions are traditional, but a mix of yellow and sweet Vidalia onions creates an even richer sweetness. Avoid red onions as they can make the broth murky.
  • Quality stock: Since the soup is essentially broth-based, the quality of your stock matters enormously. Homemade beef stock or high-quality low-sodium commercial stock will make or break this dish.
  • Cheese selection: Gruyere is the classic choice for its nutty, slightly salty flavor and excellent melting properties. Comte or Emmental are good substitutes. Avoid pre-shredded cheese as it contains anti-caking agents that prevent smooth melting.
