Classic French Croissants

Cuisine: French Prep: 45 min (active) Cook: 18 min Total: 3 hours (incl. resting and chilling) Servings: 12 croissants Difficulty: Hard Rating: 4.7 / 5 (1,542 reviews)

The croissant is the crown jewel of French viennoiserie — a category of enriched, laminated pastries that sits between bread and patisserie. While commonly associated with France, the croissant's ancestor is the Austrian kipferl, a crescent-shaped bread brought to Paris in the 19th century. French bakers transformed it by incorporating laminated dough — the painstaking technique of folding butter into dough in multiple layers — creating the shatteringly crisp, honeycomb-layered pastry we know today. A properly made croissant is an engineering marvel: 27 alternating layers of butter and dough that expand dramatically in the oven's heat, as the water in the butter converts to steam and pushes the layers apart. The exterior shatters at the first bite, giving way to a soft, slightly chewy, deeply buttery interior. This recipe, inspired by the methods of legendary French baker Christophe Vasseur, requires patience and precision but rewards you with croissants that rival the best Parisian boulangeries.

Ingredients

  • 500g bread flour (T55 French flour ideal, or King Arthur bread flour), plus extra for dusting
  • 10g fine sea salt
  • 80g granulated sugar
  • 10g instant yeast (SAF-Instant gold label recommended)
  • 300ml whole milk, cold
  • 280g unsalted European-style butter (minimum 82% fat — Plugra, Kerrygold, or President brand), cold, for the butter block
  • 30g unsalted butter, softened, for the dough
  • 1 large egg plus 1 tbsp milk, whisked together for egg wash
  • All-purpose flour for rolling surface

Step-by-Step Instructions

  1. Make the detrempe (dough): In the bowl of a stand mixer fitted with the dough hook, combine the flour, salt, sugar, and yeast. Add the cold milk and softened butter. Mix on low speed for 4 minutes until a rough dough forms, then increase to medium speed for 6 minutes until the dough is smooth, elastic, and pulls cleanly from the sides of the bowl. It should be slightly tacky but not sticky. Shape into a flat rectangle about 2cm thick, wrap tightly in plastic wrap, and refrigerate for at least 1 hour (or overnight for best results). The dough must be thoroughly chilled so it matches the butter's temperature during lamination.
  2. Prepare the butter block (beurrage): Place the 280g cold butter between two sheets of parchment paper. Using a rolling pin, pound and roll the butter into a 15cm x 15cm square of even thickness. The butter should be pliable but still cold — it should bend without cracking but not feel soft or greasy. If it gets too warm, return it to the fridge for 10 minutes. The butter and dough must be the same consistency during lamination — this is the single most important factor for success.
  3. Enclose the butter (lock-in): On a lightly floured surface, roll the chilled dough into a rectangle roughly 30cm x 16cm. Place the butter block in the center. Fold the dough flaps over the butter like an envelope, pinching the seams to seal completely — no butter should be visible. This sealed package is the foundation for lamination.
  4. First and second turns (lamination): Lightly flour the surface and roll the dough into a long rectangle approximately 50cm x 20cm, always rolling away from you in one direction and never side to side (which would tear the layers). Fold into thirds like a business letter — this is your first "single turn," creating 3 layers. Rotate the dough 90 degrees, roll out again to the same dimensions, and fold into thirds again — your second turn, creating 9 layers. Wrap in plastic and refrigerate for 30 minutes. The resting period relaxes the gluten and rechills the butter, both essential for clean layers.
  5. Third turn and final rest: Remove from the refrigerator, and perform one more single turn (roll and fold into thirds). You now have 27 layers of alternating dough and butter — the classic croissant architecture. Wrap tightly and refrigerate for at least 1 hour. At this stage, the dough can be refrigerated overnight or frozen for up to 2 weeks. If the butter broke through the dough at any point during rolling, dust the tear with flour and continue — small imperfections are recoverable.
  6. Shape the croissants: On a lightly floured surface, roll the dough into a large rectangle approximately 50cm x 25cm and about 5mm thick. Using a sharp knife or pizza cutter, cut the dough into tall isosceles triangles with a 10cm base. Make a small 1cm notch at the center of each triangle's base. Starting from the base, gently stretch the triangle to about 25cm long, then roll it up toward the point, tucking the tip underneath. Curve the ends inward to form the classic crescent shape. Place on parchment-lined baking sheets with generous spacing.
  7. Proof and egg wash: Cover loosely with plastic wrap and let proof at cool room temperature (21-24C) for 1.5 to 2 hours. The croissants are ready when they have nearly doubled in size, feel light and jiggly when the pan is gently shaken, and you can see the visible layers of dough on the sides. Do not proof in a warm place — if the butter melts during proofing, the layers will fuse and you will lose the flakiness. Gently brush with egg wash, taking care not to let it drip into the layers (which would glue them together).
  8. Bake to perfection: Preheat the oven to 200C (400F) with convection if available. Bake for 16-18 minutes, rotating the pan halfway through, until the croissants are deeply golden brown — not pale gold, but a rich mahogany. The darker color means the butter in the layers has fully caramelized, developing the complex, nutty flavor. Remove from the oven and let cool on the pan for 5 minutes before transferring to a wire rack. Resist cutting into them for at least 15 minutes — the interior continues to set as they cool. Serve warm with good coffee, a slick of French jam, or simply on their own.

Chef Tips

  • Butter quality is paramount: Use European-style butter with at least 82% butterfat (Plugra, Kerrygold, or President). Standard American butter is only 80% fat with more water, which creates steam in the wrong places and results in soggy, poorly defined layers. The higher fat content produces cleaner layer separation and a richer flavor.
  • Temperature control throughout: The butter and dough must remain cold (around 15-18C) during every step of lamination. If the dough feels warm, sticky, or the butter starts to soften, stop immediately and refrigerate for 20 minutes. Working in a cool kitchen (below 22C) is ideal. In summer, chill your rolling pin and work surface. Warm butter will absorb into the dough rather than staying in separate layers.
  • Do not add extra flour when rolling: Use only the minimum flour needed to prevent sticking. Excess flour gets trapped between the layers and creates a dry, bready texture rather than a flaky, buttery one. A bench scraper is your best friend for keeping the work surface clean and lifting the dough without adding flour.
  • The overnight method: For the best flavor development, make the dough the evening before. Refrigerate overnight after the first mix, then perform the lamination turns in the morning. The slow, cold fermentation develops complex, slightly tangy flavors that quick-risen dough cannot achieve. Shape, proof, and bake for fresh croissants by mid-morning.
  • Bake darker than you think: The most common home baker mistake is pulling croissants out too early. A pale croissant has uncooked dough in the center and under-caramelized butter. Push the color to a deep, rich golden-brown — almost the color of a hazelnut shell. This is where the full buttery, toasty flavor emerges, and the interior will be properly cooked with an open, honeycomb structure.
