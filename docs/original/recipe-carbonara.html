Spaghetti Carbonara

Cuisine: Italian Prep: 10 min Cook: 15 min Total: 25 min Servings: 4 Difficulty: Easy Rating: 4.9 / 5 (3,187 reviews)

Born in the trattorias of post-war Rome, Carbonara is a celebration of simplicity — just five ingredients transformed into a silky, savory masterpiece. The key is the emulsion: egg yolks and Pecorino Romano melt into the hot pasta, creating a luxurious cream-free sauce that clings to every strand. Forget the heavy cream versions found outside Italy; the authentic Roman preparation relies entirely on technique and the finest ingredients.

Ingredients

  • 400g spaghetti (or rigatoni for a Roman twist)
  • 200g guanciale (cured pork jowl), cut into 1cm strips
  • 6 large egg yolks plus 2 whole eggs
  • 100g Pecorino Romano DOP, finely grated (plus extra for serving)
  • 50g Parmigiano-Reggiano, finely grated
  • Freshly cracked black pepper (generous — at least 2 tsp)
  • Fine sea salt for pasta water

Step-by-Step Instructions

  1. Prepare the egg mixture: In a large bowl, whisk together the egg yolks, whole eggs, grated Pecorino Romano, and Parmigiano-Reggiano until smooth and creamy. Season with a generous amount of freshly cracked black pepper. Set aside at room temperature.
  2. Cook the guanciale: Place the guanciale strips in a cold, large skillet. Turn the heat to medium-low and render slowly for 8-10 minutes, stirring occasionally, until the fat is translucent and the edges are golden and crispy. Do not rush this step — low and slow renders the fat properly. Remove the skillet from heat and set aside. Do not drain the fat.
  3. Boil the pasta: Bring a large pot of water to a rolling boil. Salt generously (it should taste like the sea). Cook the spaghetti until 1 minute short of al dente — it will finish cooking in the sauce. Reserve 2 cups of starchy pasta water before draining.
  4. Combine pasta and guanciale: Add the drained pasta directly to the skillet with the guanciale and rendered fat. Toss over medium heat for 60 seconds, allowing the pasta to absorb the pork fat and finish cooking.
  5. Create the sauce: Remove the skillet completely from heat and let it cool for 30 seconds (this prevents scrambling). Pour the egg and cheese mixture over the hot pasta, tossing vigorously and constantly with tongs. The residual heat will gently cook the eggs into a creamy, glossy sauce that coats each strand.
  6. Adjust consistency: Add reserved pasta water one tablespoon at a time, tossing continuously, until the sauce reaches a silky, flowing consistency. It should coat the back of a spoon and pool slightly on the plate.
  7. Serve immediately: Divide among warmed plates. Top with extra grated Pecorino Romano and an aggressive shower of freshly cracked black pepper. Carbonara waits for no one — serve the moment it hits the plate.

Chef Tips

  • Guanciale vs. pancetta: Guanciale (cured pork jowl) is non-negotiable for authentic Carbonara. It has a higher fat-to-meat ratio than pancetta or bacon, rendering into a richer, more luxurious fat that forms the backbone of the sauce. Look for it at Italian delis or specialty grocers.
  • Temperature control is everything: The single most common mistake is scrambling the eggs. Always remove the pan from heat before adding the egg mixture. The pasta should be hot enough to cook the eggs gently, but not so hot that it turns into a scramble. Aim for around 70C.
  • Pasta water is liquid gold: The starch in pasta water helps emulsify the sauce. Salt your water well and save more than you think you need. Add it gradually — you can always add more, but you cannot take it away.
  • Use real Pecorino Romano DOP: Pre-grated cheese contains anti-caking agents that prevent smooth melting. Always grate fresh from a wedge. The sharp, salty tang of genuine Pecorino is irreplaceable.
  • Warm your serving plates: Carbonara cools rapidly and the sauce thickens as it sits. Warm plates in the oven at 65C for 5 minutes to keep the dish at peak consistency longer.
