Bruschetta al Pomodoro

Cuisine: Italian Prep: 10 min Cook: 5 min Total: 15 min Servings: 6 Difficulty: Easy Rating: 4.6 / 5 (1,654 reviews)

Bruschetta (pronounced "broo-SKET-tah," never "broo-SHET-tah") is the ultimate expression of Italian simplicity: grilled bread rubbed with raw garlic, drizzled with the finest olive oil, and piled high with peak-season tomatoes. This antipasto from central Italy proves that when your ingredients are exceptional, technique takes a back seat. The contrast of crunchy, smoky bread against cool, juicy tomatoes seasoned with basil and a whisper of aged balsamic is a thing of genuine beauty.

Ingredients

  • 6 large ripe Roma or heirloom tomatoes (about 600g), diced 1cm
  • 1 large loaf ciabatta or rustic Italian bread, cut into 12 slices (1.5cm thick)
  • 3 cloves garlic (2 halved for rubbing, 1 minced for topping)
  • 15 large fresh basil leaves, hand-torn
  • 4 tbsp extra-virgin olive oil (best quality you can find), plus more for drizzling
  • 1 tbsp aged balsamic vinegar (Modena, at least 6 years)
  • 1 tsp flaky sea salt (Maldon or similar)
  • Freshly cracked black pepper to taste
  • Optional: 100g fresh mozzarella or burrata, torn into small pieces

Step-by-Step Instructions

  1. Prepare the tomato topping: Dice tomatoes into 1cm pieces and place in a colander set over a bowl. Sprinkle with 1/2 tsp salt and let drain for 10 minutes to remove excess moisture. Transfer drained tomatoes to a mixing bowl. Add minced garlic, torn basil, olive oil, balsamic vinegar, and remaining salt. Toss gently and let marinate at room temperature while you prepare the bread.
  2. Grill the bread: Preheat a grill, grill pan, or broiler to high heat. Brush both sides of each bread slice lightly with olive oil. Grill for 1-2 minutes per side until you have prominent char marks and the bread is golden and crisp on the outside but still slightly chewy inside.
  3. Rub with garlic: While the bread is still hot, vigorously rub one side of each slice with a halved garlic clove. The rough, toasted surface acts as a grater, releasing garlic oils directly into the bread. Use one garlic half for every 3-4 slices.
  4. Season the bread: Drizzle the garlic-rubbed side of each slice with a generous thread of extra-virgin olive oil. Season with a tiny pinch of flaky sea salt.
  5. Assemble: Spoon the marinated tomato mixture generously onto each bread slice, using a slotted spoon to leave excess liquid behind. Pile it high — bruschetta should be abundant, not timid. If using mozzarella or burrata, add a few torn pieces on top.
  6. Finish and serve: Give each bruschetta a final drizzle of olive oil, a crack of black pepper, and a small basil leaf for garnish. Serve immediately — bruschetta waits for no one, as the bread will begin to soften within minutes.

Chef Tips

  • Tomato selection is everything: This dish lives or dies by the quality of your tomatoes. Use only ripe, in-season tomatoes — vine-ripened Roma, San Marzano, or heirloom varieties at the peak of summer. If tomatoes are not in season, frankly, make something else.
  • Drain the tomatoes: Salting and draining for 10 minutes removes excess water that would otherwise make the bread soggy. This step also seasons the tomatoes from the inside and concentrates their flavor.
  • Olive oil quality: Use the best extra-virgin olive oil you own for the finishing drizzle. A peppery, grassy Tuscan oil or a fruity Ligurian oil will add a dimension that budget oils simply cannot. This is a dish where you taste every ingredient directly.
  • Toast, then top, then eat: Never assemble bruschetta in advance. The bread must be freshly grilled and the topping added at the last moment. Even 5 minutes of sitting turns crisp bread into a soggy base.
  • The garlic rub technique: The coarse surface of grilled bread essentially grates the garlic clove as you rub it across. Press firmly and rub in broad strokes. The garlic flavor should be present but not aggressive — 3-4 passes per slice is plenty.
