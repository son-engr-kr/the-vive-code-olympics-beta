Tiramisu

Cuisine: Italian Prep: 30 min Chill: 6-24 hours Total: 30 min + chill Servings: 8 Difficulty: Easy Rating: 4.9 / 5 (4,512 reviews)

Tiramisu — "pick me up" or "lift me up" in Italian — is the country's most beloved dessert, and for good reason. Layers of espresso-soaked ladyfingers nestled between clouds of mascarpone cream, dusted with bitter cocoa. This recipe follows the traditional Veneto method, with a proper zabaglione base that gives the cream its characteristic lightness and stability. No gelatin, no whipped cream shortcuts — just the real thing, the way it has been made in Italian homes since the 1960s.

Ingredients

Showing quantities for 4 servings

  • 6 large egg yolks, at room temperature
  • 150g granulated sugar
  • 500g mascarpone cheese, at room temperature
  • 300ml strong espresso or moka pot coffee, cooled to room temperature
  • 3 tbsp Marsala wine or dark rum (or 2 tbsp of each)
  • 300-350g Savoiardi (Italian ladyfingers), about 30-36 biscuits
  • 25g unsweetened Dutch-process cocoa powder for dusting
  • 50g dark chocolate (70% cacao), finely grated (optional garnish)
  • Pinch of fine sea salt

Step-by-Step Instructions

  1. Brew and cool the coffee: Prepare 300ml of strong espresso (about 4-5 shots) or brew in a moka pot. Stir in the Marsala wine or rum while still warm, then let the mixture cool completely to room temperature. If using for children, omit the alcohol entirely.
  2. Make the zabaglione base: Set a heatproof bowl over a pot of gently simmering water (the bowl should not touch the water). Whisk egg yolks and sugar vigorously for 8-10 minutes until the mixture triples in volume, becomes pale yellow, and reaches 71C (160F). The ribbon stage is reached when the whisk leaves a trail that holds for 3 seconds. Remove from heat and let cool for 5 minutes.
  3. Fold in mascarpone: Add the mascarpone to the zabaglione in three additions, folding gently with a large rubber spatula after each. Use broad, sweeping motions from the bottom of the bowl to maintain as much air as possible. The cream should be smooth, thick, and billowy. Add a pinch of salt.
  4. Dip the Savoiardi: Pour the coffee mixture into a shallow dish. Working quickly, dip each ladyfinger into the coffee for 1-2 seconds per side. The biscuit should absorb coffee but remain structurally firm — a soggy ladyfinger will collapse. This is the most critical step: too little and the tiramisu is dry, too much and it becomes a soggy mess.
  5. Assemble the first layer: Arrange dipped ladyfingers in a single snug layer at the bottom of a 23x33cm (9x13 inch) dish or similar container. Spread half of the mascarpone cream evenly over the ladyfingers, smoothing with an offset spatula.
  6. Build the second layer: Dip and arrange a second layer of ladyfingers over the cream. Spread the remaining mascarpone cream on top, creating a smooth, even surface. The goal is two distinct layers of biscuit and cream.
  7. Chill: Cover tightly with plastic wrap (do not let it touch the surface) and refrigerate for a minimum of 6 hours, ideally 12-24 hours. This resting time allows the flavors to meld, the coffee to distribute evenly, and the mascarpone to set into a sliceable consistency.
  8. Finish and serve: Just before serving, dust the entire surface generously with cocoa powder through a fine-mesh sieve. Scatter grated dark chocolate over the top if desired. Cut into squares with a sharp knife dipped in hot water for clean edges.

Chef Tips

  • Room temperature mascarpone: Cold mascarpone is stiff and will deflate the zabaglione when folded. Let it sit at room temperature for 30 minutes before using. It should be soft and spreadable, not firm.
  • The quick dip: Savoiardi are designed to absorb liquid rapidly. A 1-2 second dip per side is all you need. Hold each biscuit horizontally and submerge only briefly. They will continue to absorb moisture from the cream as the tiramisu rests in the fridge.
  • Coffee quality matters: Use the best espresso you can make. Instant coffee produces a flat, one-dimensional flavor. A moka pot or proper espresso machine gives the bitterness and intensity that balances the sweet cream.
  • Patience pays off: Tiramisu at 6 hours is good; at 24 hours it is transcendent. The overnight rest transforms the texture from loose and layered to cohesive, sliceable, and deeply flavored throughout. Plan ahead.
  • Dust cocoa at serving time: Cocoa powder absorbs moisture and loses its visual impact if applied too early. Always dust just before bringing to the table for the most striking presentation.

Wine and Beverage Pairing

Pair with a medium-bodied Italian red such as Chianti Classico or Barbera d'Asti. For white wine lovers, a crisp Pinot Grigio or Verdicchio complements lighter dishes beautifully. Non-alcoholic option: sparkling San Pellegrino with a wedge of lemon.
