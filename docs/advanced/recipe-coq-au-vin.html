Coq au Vin

Cuisine: French Prep: 30 min Cook: 2 hours Total: 2.5 hours Servings: 6 Difficulty: Medium Rating: 4.8 / 5 (1,987 reviews)

Coq au Vin — rooster in wine — is one of the great pillars of French country cooking, immortalized by Julia Child in "Mastering the Art of French Cooking." Originally a peasant dish designed to tenderize a tough old rooster through hours of braising, it has evolved into an elegant showcase of Burgundian cuisine. Chicken pieces are marinated and braised in a full bottle of red wine until fork-tender, surrounded by smoky lardons, sweet pearl onions, and earthy mushrooms in a sauce so deeply flavored it seems impossible that it came from such humble origins. This is Sunday cooking at its finest — slow, aromatic, and immensely satisfying.

Ingredients

  • 1.8kg whole chicken, cut into 8 pieces (or 6 bone-in, skin-on thighs and drumsticks)
  • 1 bottle (750ml) Burgundy or Pinot Noir (drink-worthy quality)
  • 200g thick-cut bacon or lardons (preferably slab bacon, cut into 1cm batons)
  • 250g pearl onions, peeled (or frozen pearl onions, thawed)
  • 250g cremini mushrooms, quartered
  • 2 medium carrots, cut into 3cm pieces
  • 3 cloves garlic, minced
  • 2 tbsp tomato paste
  • 2 tbsp all-purpose flour
  • 250ml chicken stock
  • 2 tbsp cognac or brandy
  • 1 bouquet garni (3 sprigs thyme, 2 bay leaves, 6 parsley stems, tied with kitchen twine)
  • 2 tbsp unsalted butter
  • 2 tbsp extra-virgin olive oil
  • 2 tbsp fresh flat-leaf parsley, chopped (for garnish)
  • Fine sea salt and freshly ground black pepper

Step-by-Step Instructions

  1. Marinate the chicken (optional but recommended): Place chicken pieces in a large bowl or zip-lock bag. Pour in half the bottle of wine, add 2 smashed garlic cloves, a few thyme sprigs, and a bay leaf. Refrigerate for 4-12 hours. When ready to cook, remove chicken, pat completely dry with paper towels (wet skin will not brown), and strain and reserve the marinade. If skipping marination, simply use the full bottle when braising.
  2. Render the lardons: In a large Dutch oven or heavy-bottomed pot, cook lardons over medium heat for 8-10 minutes, stirring occasionally, until crispy and golden with rendered fat. Remove with a slotted spoon and set aside. Leave the fat in the pot.
  3. Brown the chicken: Season chicken generously with salt and pepper. Working in batches to avoid crowding, sear chicken skin-side down in the lardon fat over medium-high heat for 4-5 minutes until the skin is deeply golden and crispy. Flip and brown the other side for 3 minutes. Remove and set aside. Do not rush this step — deep browning creates the foundation of the sauce's flavor through the Maillard reaction.
  4. Saute the aromatics: Pour off all but 2 tablespoons of fat. Add pearl onions and cook 5 minutes until lightly browned. Add mushrooms and cook 4 minutes until golden. Add carrots and minced garlic, cook 1 minute. Sprinkle flour over the vegetables and stir for 1 minute to cook out the raw taste. Add tomato paste and stir for 30 seconds.
  5. Deglaze and braise: Pour in the cognac and carefully ignite with a long match (or simply let it boil off for 1 minute). Add the reserved marinade and remaining wine, chicken stock, and bouquet garni. Bring to a simmer, scraping up all the browned bits (fond) from the bottom — this is concentrated flavor. Nestle the chicken pieces back in, skin-side up, so the skin stays above the liquid. The liquid should come about three-quarters up the chicken.
  6. Slow braise: Cover the pot with the lid slightly ajar and transfer to a preheated oven at 160C (325F). Braise for 1.5-2 hours until the chicken is completely tender and nearly falling off the bone. The sauce should have reduced and thickened naturally.
  7. Finish the sauce: Carefully remove chicken pieces to a warm platter. Discard the bouquet garni. If the sauce is too thin, simmer uncovered on the stovetop for 10-15 minutes until it coats the back of a spoon. Whisk in 2 tablespoons of cold butter (monter au beurre) for richness and gloss. Return the lardons to the sauce. Taste and adjust seasoning.
  8. Serve: Arrange chicken on warmed plates or a large serving dish. Spoon the sauce, mushrooms, onions, and lardons over and around the chicken. Garnish with fresh chopped parsley. Serve with crusty bread, buttered egg noodles, or creamy mashed potatoes to soak up every drop of sauce.

Chef Tips

  • Use wine you would drink: The old adage holds true — never cook with wine you would not drink. A decent Burgundy or Pinot Noir (in the 10-15 USD range) provides the fruity, earthy backbone this dish demands. Cooking wine from a supermarket shelf will produce a flat, acidic sauce.
  • Dry the chicken thoroughly: Moisture is the enemy of browning. After marinating, pat each piece bone-dry with paper towels. Wet skin will steam rather than sear, and you will miss out on the Maillard reaction that builds the deepest flavors in the dish.
  • Make it a day ahead: Like most braises, Coq au Vin improves dramatically after resting overnight in the refrigerator. The flavors meld and deepen, and reheating gently on the stovetop produces a more cohesive, richer result. It is actually better on day two.
  • Monter au beurre: Whisking cold butter into the finished sauce (mounting with butter) is a classic French technique that adds body, gloss, and richness. Use cold butter and whisk off the heat — warm butter will break and become greasy instead of emulsifying.
  • Peeling pearl onions: Blanch pearl onions in boiling water for 30 seconds, then plunge into ice water. The skins will slip off easily. Alternatively, use frozen pearl onions — they are pre-peeled and perfectly fine for braising.
