Mushroom Risotto

Cuisine: Italian Prep: 15 min Cook: 25 min Total: 40 min Servings: 4 Difficulty: Medium Rating: 4.7 / 5 (1,892 reviews)

Risotto ai funghi is Northern Italy's answer to comfort food — a dish that rewards patience and attention. Originating in the Po Valley where rice paddies stretch across the Lombardy and Piedmont plains, risotto is built one ladle at a time, each addition of broth coaxing starch from the grain until the result is impossibly creamy without a drop of cream. The earthy depth of dried porcini combined with fresh cremini mushrooms creates a woodsy, umami-rich masterpiece.

Ingredients

  • 30g dried porcini mushrooms
  • 300g cremini (baby bella) mushrooms, sliced 5mm thick
  • 1.2 liters chicken or vegetable stock, kept warm on a back burner
  • 320g Arborio or Carnaroli rice (do not rinse)
  • 1 medium shallot, finely diced
  • 3 cloves garlic, minced
  • 150ml dry white wine (Pinot Grigio or Soave)
  • 60g unsalted butter, divided (40g for cooking, 20g for mantecatura)
  • 2 tbsp extra-virgin olive oil
  • 80g Parmigiano-Reggiano, finely grated (plus extra for serving)
  • 2 tbsp fresh flat-leaf parsley, finely chopped
  • 1 tsp fresh thyme leaves
  • Fine sea salt and freshly ground black pepper to taste

Step-by-Step Instructions

  1. Rehydrate the porcini: Place dried porcini in a bowl and cover with 250ml boiling water. Let soak for 20 minutes. Remove mushrooms, squeeze dry, and roughly chop. Strain the soaking liquid through a fine-mesh sieve lined with cheesecloth to remove grit. Add the liquid to your warm stock — this is concentrated umami gold.
  2. Saute the mushrooms: Heat olive oil and 20g butter in a large, wide skillet or saucepan over medium-high heat. Add cremini mushrooms in a single layer (work in batches to avoid steaming). Cook undisturbed for 3 minutes until golden, flip, and cook 2 minutes more. Add rehydrated porcini and thyme, season with salt, and cook 1 minute. Transfer to a plate.
  3. Build the base: In the same pan, melt 20g butter over medium heat. Add shallot and cook 3 minutes until soft and translucent. Add garlic and cook 30 seconds until fragrant. Add the rice and stir constantly for 2 minutes, toasting until the edges become translucent and the center stays opaque — this is the tostatura, essential for texture.
  4. Deglaze with wine: Pour in the white wine and stir until it is completely absorbed. The alcohol will cook off, leaving behind acidity and depth that balances the richness.
  5. Add stock gradually: Begin adding warm stock one ladle (about 120ml) at a time, stirring frequently. Wait until each addition is nearly absorbed before adding the next. Maintain a gentle, steady simmer — never a rapid boil. This process takes 18-20 minutes. The rice is done when it is tender but still has a slight bite at the core (al onda — it should flow like a wave when you tilt the pan).
  6. Mantecatura (the final emulsion): Remove from heat. Add the remaining 20g cold butter and grated Parmigiano-Reggiano. Stir vigorously for 60 seconds — this step creates the signature creamy, glossy consistency. Fold in the sauteed mushrooms.
  7. Rest and serve: Cover and let rest 2 minutes. The risotto should spread slowly when spooned onto a warm plate — Italians call this all'onda. Garnish with parsley, extra Parmigiano, and a few grinds of black pepper. Serve immediately in warmed shallow bowls.

Chef Tips

  • Carnaroli vs. Arborio: Professional Italian chefs prefer Carnaroli rice — it has a higher starch content and a firmer core, making it more forgiving and producing an even creamier result. Arborio works well, but Carnaroli is the gold standard for risotto.
  • Never rinse the rice: The surface starch on the grains is what creates the creamy consistency. Rinsing it away defeats the purpose of the entire technique.
  • Keep the stock hot: Adding cold stock to hot rice shocks the grains and slows cooking, resulting in uneven texture. Always keep your stock at a gentle simmer on a nearby burner.
  • Stirring technique: You do not need to stir constantly, but frequently. Stir in a figure-eight pattern every 30-45 seconds. Obsessive stirring breaks down the grains too much, while neglecting it causes sticking.
  • The cold butter trick: During mantecatura, cold butter emulsifies better than room-temperature butter. Cut it into small cubes before adding for the smoothest result.
