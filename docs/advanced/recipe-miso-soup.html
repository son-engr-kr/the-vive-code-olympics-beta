Traditional Miso Soup

Cuisine: Japanese Total: 15 min Servings: 4 Difficulty: Easy Rating: 4.6 / 5

Miso soup is the heart of the Japanese table, served with nearly every meal from breakfast to dinner. This deceptively simple dish relies on high-quality dashi stock and properly dissolved miso paste to achieve its signature balance of savory umami depth and gentle warmth. Mastering miso soup means mastering the fundamentals of Japanese cooking.

Ingredients

  • 800ml water
  • 10g kombu (dried kelp), about a 10cm piece
  • 15g katsuobushi (bonito flakes)
  • 3 tbsp white miso paste (shiro miso)
  • 1 tbsp red miso paste (aka miso), optional for depth
  • 150g silken tofu, cut into 1.5cm cubes
  • 2 tbsp dried wakame seaweed
  • 2 green onions, thinly sliced
  • 1 tsp dashi granules (optional, for extra depth)

Step-by-Step Instructions

  1. Prepare the dashi: Place kombu in a pot with 800ml of cold water. Heat over medium heat until small bubbles appear around the edges, about 8 minutes. Remove the kombu just before the water reaches a full boil to prevent bitterness.
  2. Add bonito flakes: Once the water reaches a gentle boil, add katsuobushi. Let them steep for 30 seconds without stirring, then immediately strain through a fine-mesh sieve lined with cheesecloth. Discard the solids. This is your ichiban dashi.
  3. Rehydrate wakame: While making dashi, soak dried wakame in a small bowl of cold water for 5 minutes. Drain and set aside. The seaweed will expand to several times its dried volume.
  4. Dissolve the miso: Return the dashi to the pot over low heat. Place the miso paste in a ladle, submerge it partially in the broth, and whisk with chopsticks until fully dissolved. Never boil the soup after adding miso, as boiling destroys the beneficial enzymes and dulls the flavor.
  5. Add tofu and wakame: Gently slide the tofu cubes into the soup along with the drained wakame. Heat through for 1-2 minutes over low heat without stirring vigorously, as silken tofu breaks apart easily.
  6. Serve: Ladle into individual bowls and garnish with sliced green onions. Serve immediately while hot alongside steamed rice and pickled vegetables.

Chef Tips

  • Never boil miso: The most critical rule. Boiling miso destroys its live cultures, dulls the flavor, and makes it smell harsh. Always add miso off the heat or over very low heat.
  • Miso varieties: White (shiro) miso is mild and slightly sweet, perfect for everyday soup. Red (aka) miso is deeper and saltier. Mixing the two in a 2:1 ratio creates a well-rounded awase miso flavor.
  • Homemade dashi is worth it: Instant dashi granules work in a pinch, but homemade dashi from kombu and katsuobushi has a cleaner, more nuanced flavor that elevates the entire soup.
  • Seasonal variations: Add thinly sliced mushrooms, daikon, spinach, or clams to vary the soup with the seasons. In Japan, miso soup ingredients change throughout the year to reflect seasonal produce.
  • Tofu handling: Use silken tofu for the most authentic texture. Cut it with a wet knife and slide pieces off the blade gently to avoid breaking them.
