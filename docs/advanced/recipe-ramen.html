Tonkotsu Ramen

Cuisine: Japanese Prep: 30 min Cook: 3.5 hours Total: 4 hours Servings: 4 Difficulty: Hard Rating: 4.8 / 5 (2,876 reviews)

Tonkotsu ramen — the milky-white, collagen-rich pork bone broth that put Fukuoka on the world culinary map. This is not a shortcut recipe. Real tonkotsu demands hours of vigorous boiling to shatter the pork bones and emulsify their marrow fat and collagen into a broth so rich it coats your lips. Paired with thin, straight noodles, melt-in-your-mouth chashu pork belly, and a jammy marinated egg, this bowl is the culmination of Japanese ramen craft. The effort is immense; the reward is transcendent.

Ingredients

Showing quantities for 4 servings

  • Broth:
  • 1.5kg pork leg bones (femur), split lengthwise by your butcher
  • 500g pork trotters (feet), halved
  • 200g pork fatback or skin
  • 1 large onion, halved
  • 1 head of garlic, halved crosswise
  • 5cm piece of fresh ginger, sliced
  • 3 liters water (plus more as needed)
  • Tare (seasoning base):
  • 4 tbsp white soy sauce (shiro shoyu) or regular soy sauce
  • 2 tbsp sake
  • 1 tbsp mirin
  • 1 tsp fine sea salt
  • Chashu pork belly:
  • 500g pork belly, skin-on, rolled and tied with kitchen twine
  • 100ml soy sauce
  • 100ml mirin
  • 50ml sake
  • 2 tbsp sugar
  • 3 slices ginger
  • 2 cloves garlic, smashed
  • Ajitsuke tamago (marinated eggs):
  • 4 large eggs
  • 100ml soy sauce
  • 100ml mirin
  • 100ml water
  • Toppings:
  • 4 portions fresh ramen noodles (thin, straight — Hakata style)
  • 4 sheets toasted nori
  • 4 tbsp finely sliced green onions (scallions)
  • Mayu (black garlic oil) or chili oil to taste
  • White sesame seeds
  • Pickled red ginger (beni shoga)

Step-by-Step Instructions

  1. Blanch the bones: Place pork leg bones and trotters in a large stockpot, cover with cold water, and bring to a rolling boil. Boil for 15 minutes to release impurities. Drain, discard the water, and rinse each bone thoroughly under cold running water, scrubbing away any dark bits or coagulated blood. This step is essential for a clean, white broth.
  2. Build the broth: Return cleaned bones and trotters to the pot with fatback, onion, garlic head, and ginger. Add 3 liters of fresh water. Bring to a vigorous, rolling boil over high heat. Once boiling, reduce heat to maintain a strong, active boil (not a gentle simmer — the aggressive boiling is what emulsifies the fat and collagen). Cook for 3-3.5 hours, stirring occasionally and adding boiling water as needed to keep the bones submerged. The broth should turn opaque and milky white.
  3. Make the chashu (while broth simmers): Sear the rolled pork belly in a hot pan on all sides until golden brown. In a small saucepan, combine soy sauce, mirin, sake, sugar, ginger, and garlic. Bring to a simmer, add the seared pork belly, cover with a drop lid (otoshibuta) or parchment paper, and braise gently for 2 hours until fork-tender. Let cool in the braising liquid. Reserve the liquid for the eggs.
  4. Prepare the marinated eggs: Bring a pot of water to a boil. Gently lower eggs in and cook for exactly 6 minutes and 30 seconds for a jammy yolk. Transfer immediately to an ice bath for 5 minutes, then peel carefully. Mix soy sauce, mirin, and water in a container. Add peeled eggs and refrigerate for at least 4 hours, ideally overnight, turning occasionally for even coloring.
  5. Finish the broth: After 3-3.5 hours, the broth should be thick, milky, and richly flavored. Strain through a fine-mesh sieve, pressing on the solids to extract maximum flavor. Discard bones and aromatics. You should have about 1.5-2 liters of concentrated broth. Keep warm.
  6. Prepare the tare: Mix white soy sauce, sake, mirin, and salt in a small saucepan. Warm gently to combine flavors. Place 2 tablespoons of tare in the bottom of each serving bowl.
  7. Cook the noodles: Bring a large pot of unsalted water to a rolling boil. Cook fresh ramen noodles according to package directions (usually 60-90 seconds for thin Hakata-style). Drain thoroughly, shaking off excess water.
  8. Assemble each bowl: Ladle 350ml of hot broth into each tare-seasoned bowl and stir to combine. Add drained noodles in a neat bundle. Slice chashu pork belly into 5mm rounds and arrange 3-4 slices on one side. Halve a marinated egg and place yolk-side up. Add a sheet of nori, a mound of sliced scallions, a drizzle of mayu or chili oil, a sprinkle of sesame seeds, and a pinch of beni shoga. Serve immediately.

Chef Tips

  • Boil aggressively, not gently: Unlike French stock-making where clarity demands a gentle simmer, tonkotsu requires the opposite. The violent, rolling boil breaks down collagen and emulsifies fat into the water, creating the signature milky-white opacity. If your broth is clear, your heat is too low.
  • Split the bones: Ask your butcher to split the femur bones lengthwise. This exposes the marrow, which dissolves into the broth and adds body and richness that whole bones cannot achieve in the same timeframe.
  • The 6:30 egg: Exactly 6 minutes and 30 seconds from a rolling boil produces the ideal ramen egg — a fully set white with a deep orange, jammy yolk that oozes when halved. Start timing from the moment the eggs enter the water, and use a timer. Even 30 seconds changes the result dramatically.
  • Tare in the bowl, not the pot: Season each bowl individually with tare, not the entire pot of broth. This allows guests to adjust saltiness and lets you store unseasoned broth in the fridge for up to 5 days or freeze it for months.
  • Make it a two-day project: The broth and chashu can (and should) be made a day ahead. Refrigerate overnight — the fat cap that solidifies on top can be removed or stirred back in, and the flavors deepen considerably. Reheat on the stove before serving.
