Tom Yum Soup

Cuisine: Thai Prep: 10 min Cook: 15 min Total: 25 min Servings: 4 Difficulty: Easy Rating: 4.7 / 5 (2,134 reviews)

Tom Yum Goong is Thailand's most famous soup and a UNESCO-recognized cultural treasure. "Tom" means boil, "Yum" means mixed sour-spicy salad — together, they describe a broth that hits every taste receptor simultaneously: sour lime, fiery chiles, aromatic lemongrass, earthy galangal, and savory shrimp. This is the "nam sai" (clear broth) version, where the aromatics infuse the broth directly without coconut milk, creating an intensely flavorful, light, and clean soup that wakes up every sense. It is Thailand in a bowl — bold, bright, and completely addictive.

Ingredients

Showing quantities for 4 servings

  • 400g large shrimp (prawns), shell-on (16-20 count), peeled and deveined (save the shells)
  • 1 liter chicken stock or water
  • 3 stalks lemongrass, bottom 10cm only, cut into 5cm pieces and bruised with the back of a knife
  • 5cm piece of galangal, sliced into thin coins (do not substitute ginger — they are different)
  • 5 makrut (kaffir) lime leaves, torn in half with center vein removed
  • 200g straw mushrooms, halved (or oyster mushrooms, torn into pieces)
  • 3 Roma tomatoes, cut into wedges
  • 5-8 Thai bird's eye chiles (prik kee noo), lightly crushed
  • 3 tbsp fish sauce (nam pla), plus more to taste
  • 3 tbsp fresh lime juice (about 2-3 limes), plus more to taste
  • 1 tsp sugar (to balance)
  • 2 tbsp Thai roasted chile paste (nam prik pao)
  • Fresh cilantro leaves for garnish
  • 2 scallions, thinly sliced

Step-by-Step Instructions

  1. Make shrimp stock: Place the reserved shrimp shells in a saucepan with 1 liter of chicken stock or water. Bring to a boil, then simmer for 10 minutes. The shells release a tremendous amount of shrimp flavor and color into the broth. Strain and discard the shells. This simple step elevates the soup from good to extraordinary.
  2. Build the aromatic base: Bring the shrimp-enriched stock to a boil. Add the bruised lemongrass, galangal slices, and torn makrut lime leaves. Boil for 3-4 minutes — these aromatics are not meant to be eaten (they are too fibrous) but they infuse the broth with their essential oils. The kitchen should smell intensely fragrant at this point.
  3. Add vegetables: Add the straw mushrooms, tomato wedges, and crushed bird's eye chiles. Simmer for 2 minutes until the mushrooms are just tender and the tomatoes begin to soften. The tomatoes add natural acidity and body to the broth.
  4. Cook the shrimp: Add the peeled shrimp to the simmering broth. Cook for 2-3 minutes until the shrimp curl into a C-shape and turn pink. Do not overcook — shrimp go from perfect to rubbery in under a minute. Remove the pot from heat immediately once they are done.
  5. Season the soup: Stir in the fish sauce, nam prik pao (roasted chile paste), sugar, and lime juice. Taste carefully and adjust: the soup should be aggressively sour from the lime, salty from the fish sauce, and spicy from the chiles, with a slight sweetness that ties everything together. The flavors should be bold and punchy, not gentle.
  6. Final adjustment: Thai cooks always taste and adjust at the end. Add more lime juice for sourness, more fish sauce for saltiness, more chiles for heat, or a pinch of sugar if the sour-spicy balance feels too sharp. Every bowl should hit the perfect equilibrium for your palate.
  7. Serve: Ladle into deep bowls, making sure each serving gets plenty of shrimp, mushrooms, and tomatoes. Garnish with fresh cilantro leaves and sliced scallions. Serve immediately with steamed jasmine rice on the side — spoon the rice into the soup or eat alongside. Do not eat the lemongrass, galangal, or lime leaves.

Chef Tips

  • Galangal is not ginger: Despite looking similar, galangal has a completely different flavor profile — piney, citrusy, and sharp compared to ginger's warm heat. Substituting ginger will produce a fundamentally different soup. Find galangal at Asian grocery stores; it freezes well for months.
  • Save and use the shrimp shells: Never throw away shrimp shells when making Tom Yum. Boiling them for just 10 minutes extracts an incredible depth of seafood flavor that water or plain stock cannot provide. This is the professional Thai restaurant secret for deeply flavored Tom Yum.
  • Nam prik pao is the secret ingredient: Thai roasted chile paste (nam prik pao) adds a smoky, slightly sweet complexity that rounds out the sharp sour-spicy notes. It is the difference between a one-dimensional hot-sour broth and a complex, layered soup. Find it in jars at Asian markets — Mae Pranom brand is excellent.
  • Add lime juice off heat: Lime juice added to actively boiling liquid loses its bright freshness and develops a cooked, bitter taste. Always add it after removing the pot from heat to preserve the vibrant, punchy sourness that defines great Tom Yum.
  • Clear vs. creamy (nam sai vs. nam khon): This recipe is the clear "nam sai" version. For the creamy "nam khon" style popular in many Thai restaurants, stir in 3-4 tablespoons of evaporated milk or coconut milk at the end. Both versions are authentic; it is purely a matter of preference.

Drink Pairing

The heat and complexity of Thai cuisine pairs beautifully with an off-dry German Riesling (Spatlese) or a cold Singha beer. Thai iced tea (cha yen) -- strongly brewed tea with condensed milk -- is the quintessential local accompaniment.
