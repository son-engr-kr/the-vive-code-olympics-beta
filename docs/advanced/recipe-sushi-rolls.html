Salmon Sushi Rolls

Cuisine: Japanese Prep: 40 min Cook: 20 min Total: 60 min Servings: 4 Difficulty: Medium Rating: 4.8 / 5 (1,876 reviews)

Sushi is one of the most iconic dishes in Japanese cuisine, with origins tracing back to the Edo period in the early 19th century when Hanaya Yohei pioneered nigiri-zushi in Tokyo. The maki roll — rice and fillings wrapped in nori seaweed — emerged as a practical way to enjoy sushi by hand. This salmon maki recipe features buttery, sushi-grade salmon paired with perfectly seasoned vinegared rice, crisp cucumber, and creamy avocado. Mastering sushi at home is deeply rewarding, and once you learn the fundamentals of rice preparation and rolling technique, you can experiment endlessly with fillings and presentations. The key lies in the rice: slightly warm, well-seasoned, and never packed too tightly.

Ingredients

Showing quantities for 4 servings

  • 300g sushi-grade salmon fillet (Ora King or Scottish salmon recommended), skinned and sliced into 1cm strips
  • 400g Japanese short-grain sushi rice (Koshihikari variety preferred)
  • 480ml cold water for cooking rice
  • 80ml rice vinegar (Mizkan brand recommended)
  • 2 tbsp granulated sugar
  • 1 tsp fine sea salt
  • 5 sheets nori seaweed (full-size, toasted grade A)
  • 1 ripe Hass avocado, sliced into thin strips
  • 1 Japanese cucumber (or English cucumber), cut into long matchsticks
  • 2 tbsp toasted white sesame seeds
  • Soy sauce (Kikkoman naturally brewed), pickled ginger, and wasabi for serving
  • 1 bamboo sushi rolling mat, wrapped in plastic wrap

Step-by-Step Instructions

  1. Prepare the sushi rice: Rinse the rice in a fine-mesh strainer under cold running water, gently agitating with your fingers, until the water runs mostly clear (about 4-5 rinses). This removes excess surface starch that would make the rice gummy. Transfer to a rice cooker or heavy-bottomed pot with 480ml water. If using a pot, bring to a boil over high heat, then reduce to the lowest setting, cover tightly, and cook for 15 minutes. Remove from heat and let steam, covered, for an additional 10 minutes without lifting the lid.
  2. Season the rice: While the rice cooks, combine rice vinegar, sugar, and salt in a small saucepan over low heat, stirring until dissolved — do not boil. Transfer the hot cooked rice to a large wooden hangiri (sushi tub) or wide non-metallic bowl. Drizzle the vinegar mixture over the rice and use a rice paddle or flat wooden spatula to fold and cut through the rice with slicing motions (never stir) while fanning to cool it quickly. This gives the rice its characteristic glossy sheen and slightly sticky yet firm texture. The rice should be body temperature when you begin rolling.
  3. Prepare the fillings: Slice the salmon against the grain into strips approximately 1cm wide and the length of the nori sheet. Cut the avocado in half, remove the pit, and slice each half into thin even strips. Cut the cucumber into long matchsticks about 5mm wide. Pat the salmon dry with paper towels and keep all fillings within easy reach on your cutting board.
  4. Set up your rolling station: Place the bamboo mat (wrapped in plastic wrap) on a clean, dry surface. Prepare a small bowl of tezu — water mixed with a splash of rice vinegar — to keep your hands moistened so the rice does not stick. Have a sharp, wet knife ready for cutting. Lay a sheet of nori, shiny side down, on the bamboo mat with the longer edge facing you.
  5. Assemble the roll: Wet your hands with the tezu mixture. Spread about 150g of sushi rice evenly over the nori, leaving a 2cm strip bare along the top edge. The rice layer should be thin — about 5mm — and even. Avoid pressing too hard. Lay 2-3 strips of salmon horizontally across the center of the rice. Add a line of avocado slices and cucumber matchsticks beside the salmon. Sprinkle a pinch of sesame seeds along the fillings.
  6. Roll the sushi: Using the bamboo mat, lift the edge nearest to you and fold it over the fillings, tucking firmly but gently to form a tight cylinder. Continue rolling forward with even pressure, using the mat to shape the roll into a uniform round or slightly squared shape. When you reach the bare nori strip, moisten it lightly with water to seal. Give the completed roll a gentle squeeze through the mat to compact it.
  7. Cut and present: Using a very sharp knife dampened with water (wipe and re-wet between every cut), trim the rough ends off the roll, then cut into 6-8 equal pieces using a single, confident pulling motion rather than a sawing action. Arrange the pieces cut-side up on a plate. Repeat with remaining nori sheets and fillings.
  8. Serve: Present the sushi rolls on a clean platter or individual plates alongside small dishes of soy sauce, a mound of pickled ginger, and a small dab of freshly grated wasabi. Garnish with extra sesame seeds and thin scallion rings if desired. Serve immediately — sushi is best enjoyed within 30 minutes of rolling while the nori retains its crispness and the rice is at the ideal temperature.

Chef Tips

  • Sushi-grade freshness: Always buy salmon labeled "sushi-grade" or "sashimi-grade" from a trusted fishmonger. The fish should have been flash-frozen to at least -20C for 7 days (or -35C for 15 hours) to eliminate parasites. It should smell clean and oceanic, never fishy. If in doubt, ask your fishmonger when the fish was received and how it was handled.
  • Rice is everything: The rice is the soul of sushi — it should be slightly warm, glossy, and seasoned so that each grain holds together without being mushy. Use a wooden tub (hangiri) if possible, as the wood absorbs excess moisture. Never refrigerate sushi rice; it becomes hard and loses its texture. Always make rice fresh for each batch of sushi.
  • Knife technique: A razor-sharp, wet knife is essential for clean cuts. Dip the blade in water before every single cut. Use one long drawing stroke rather than pressing straight down, which compresses the roll. A dull knife will crush the roll and tear the nori, ruining the presentation.
  • Nori quality matters: Use grade-A toasted nori that is dark green-black, crisp, and has a subtle roasted aroma. Cheap nori tends to be chewy and tough. Store unused sheets in a sealed container with a desiccant pack, as nori absorbs moisture from the air quickly and becomes limp.
  • Creative variations: Once you master the basic roll, try inside-out rolls (uramaki) with rice on the outside coated in tobiko or sesame seeds. You can also experiment with fillings like spicy tuna with sriracha mayo, tempura shrimp for crunch, or cream cheese and smoked salmon for a Philadelphia roll.
