Tacos al Pastor

Cuisine: Mexican Prep: 30 min Cook: 60 min Total: 90 min Servings: 6 Difficulty: Medium Rating: 4.9 / 5 (3,214 reviews)

Tacos al Pastor is one of Mexico's most celebrated street foods, born from a fascinating fusion of cultures. In the early 20th century, Lebanese immigrants brought shawarma-style vertical spit cooking to Mexico, particularly to Puebla and Mexico City. Mexican cooks adapted the technique by replacing lamb with pork and introducing indigenous ingredients — dried chiles, achiote paste, and pineapple — creating something entirely new. The result is a masterpiece of charred, caramelized pork shaved from a rotating trompo, topped with sweet grilled pineapple, raw white onion, fresh cilantro, and a squeeze of lime, all cradled in a soft, warm corn tortilla. While a traditional trompo is impractical at home, this recipe faithfully recreates the flavors using an oven-broiling technique that produces beautifully charred, deeply flavorful meat with those signature crispy edges.

Ingredients

  • 1kg boneless pork shoulder, sliced into 5mm-thick sheets against the grain
  • 4 dried guajillo chiles, stemmed and seeded
  • 2 dried ancho chiles, stemmed and seeded
  • 2 chipotle peppers in adobo sauce (La Costena or San Marcos brand)
  • 3 tbsp achiote paste (El Yucateco recommended)
  • 4 cloves garlic, peeled
  • 1/2 cup fresh pineapple juice plus 1/2 fresh pineapple, cut into 1cm rings
  • 2 tbsp white vinegar
  • 1 tsp ground cumin, 1 tsp dried Mexican oregano, 1/2 tsp ground cloves
  • 18 small corn tortillas (fresh masa tortillas if available, or high-quality store-bought)
  • 1 white onion, finely diced, plus extra sliced for grilling
  • 1 large bunch fresh cilantro, roughly chopped
  • 4 limes, quartered, and your favorite salsa verde for serving

Step-by-Step Instructions

  1. Toast and rehydrate the chiles: Heat a dry comal or cast-iron skillet over medium-high heat. Press the guajillo and ancho chiles flat against the hot surface for 15-20 seconds per side until fragrant and slightly pliable — be careful not to burn them, as this creates bitterness. Transfer the toasted chiles to a bowl and cover with boiling water. Let them soak for 20 minutes until completely softened, then drain, reserving 1/4 cup of the soaking liquid for the marinade.
  2. Prepare the al pastor marinade: In a blender, combine the rehydrated chiles, chipotle peppers with a spoonful of their adobo sauce, achiote paste, garlic, pineapple juice, white vinegar, cumin, oregano, cloves, 1 teaspoon salt, 1/2 teaspoon black pepper, and the reserved soaking liquid. Blend on high for 2 minutes until completely smooth. The marinade should be a deep red-orange, thick but pourable. Taste and adjust seasoning — it should be complex, slightly smoky, and just a touch sweet from the pineapple.
  3. Marinate the pork: Place the thinly sliced pork in a large bowl or zip-lock bag. Pour the marinade over the meat and massage it thoroughly, ensuring every piece is evenly coated. Cover and refrigerate for at least 2 hours, or ideally overnight (up to 24 hours). The longer it marinates, the deeper the flavors penetrate. Remove from the refrigerator 30 minutes before cooking to take off the chill.
  4. Cook the pork (oven-broil method): Preheat your oven's broiler to high and position a rack 15cm from the heating element. Line a large baking sheet with aluminum foil. Arrange the marinated pork slices in a single layer on the sheet — work in batches to avoid overlapping. Broil for 5-7 minutes per side, watching carefully, until the edges are charred and caramelized with blackened spots while the centers remain juicy. The high heat mimics the searing surface of a trompo. Let rest for 3 minutes, then chop into small pieces on a cutting board.
  5. Grill the pineapple: While the pork rests, place the pineapple rings under the broiler or on a hot grill pan. Cook for 2-3 minutes per side until you get deep caramel-brown grill marks and the sugars begin to bubble. The heat concentrates the pineapple's sweetness and adds a subtle smoky note that is essential to authentic al pastor. Chop the grilled pineapple into small dice.
  6. Warm the tortillas: Heat a dry comal or cast-iron skillet over medium-high heat. Warm each tortilla for 30-45 seconds per side until soft, pliable, and lightly spotted with brown. Stack them in a clean kitchen towel or tortilla warmer to keep them steaming hot. For extra authenticity, double-stack the tortillas — two per taco — which is how they are served at taquerias across Mexico City.
  7. Assemble the tacos: Place a generous portion of the chopped, charred pork onto each warm double-stacked tortilla. Top with diced grilled pineapple, finely diced white onion, and a generous pinch of fresh cilantro. Squeeze a wedge of lime over each taco and add a spoonful of salsa verde. The combination of smoky, spicy pork with sweet pineapple, sharp onion, herbaceous cilantro, and bright lime is what makes al pastor one of the world's great street foods.
  8. Serve immediately: Arrange the tacos on a large platter with extra lime wedges, sliced radishes, and small bowls of salsa verde and salsa roja on the side. Serve with Mexican rice and refried beans for a complete meal, or enjoy them standing up at the counter with an ice-cold Mexican lager or agua de jamaica, just as they do at the taquerias of Mexico City's streets.

Chef Tips

  • Thin slicing is crucial: The pork must be sliced very thin (5mm or less) to mimic the paper-thin shavings from a traditional trompo. Pop the pork shoulder in the freezer for 30-45 minutes before slicing — the firmer texture makes it dramatically easier to cut thin, even sheets. Ask your butcher to do this if your knife skills are not up to it.
  • Achiote paste is non-negotiable: Achiote paste (made from annatto seeds) gives al pastor its signature red-orange color and earthy, slightly peppery flavor. It is available at Latin grocery stores or online. Do not substitute with paprika — the flavor profile is completely different. El Yucateco is the most widely available and reliable brand.
  • Don't skip the pineapple: The grilled pineapple is not just a garnish — it is a structural component of the flavor. The bromelain enzyme in fresh pineapple tenderizes the pork during marination, while the caramelized sugars from grilling provide a sweet counterpoint to the smoky, spicy meat. Use fresh pineapple, never canned, for best results.
  • Tortilla quality: The tortilla is the foundation. Freshly made masa tortillas from a Mexican bakery or tortilleria are transformative — the corn flavor is incomparably richer. If using store-bought, choose brands made with nixtamalized corn (look for "masa harina" or "nixtamal" in the ingredients). Warm them properly; a cold, stiff tortilla ruins even the best filling.
  • Char is flavor: Do not fear the blackened edges on the pork. Those charred, crispy bits are where the magic happens — the Maillard reaction on the chile-achiote marinade creates incredibly complex, smoky flavors. If your broiler is not getting the job done, finish the pork in a screaming-hot cast-iron skillet to develop extra crust.
