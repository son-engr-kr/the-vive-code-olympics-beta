Butter Chicken (Murgh Makhani)

Cuisine: Indian Prep: 20 min Cook: 30 min Total: 50 min Servings: 4 Difficulty: Easy Rating: 4.8 / 5 (2,987 reviews)

Butter Chicken, known as Murgh Makhani in Hindi, was invented in the 1950s at Moti Mahal restaurant in Delhi by Kundan Lal Gujral. The story goes that leftover tandoori chicken was simmered in a rich gravy of tomatoes, butter, and cream to prevent it from drying out — and a legend was born. Today it is arguably the most beloved Indian dish worldwide, and for good reason: the sauce is a symphony of warm spices, tangy tomatoes, and luxurious cream, perfectly balanced so that no single element dominates. The chicken, ideally marinated in yogurt and spices before a quick sear, emerges tender and infused with flavor. This recipe stays true to the original Moti Mahal tradition while being entirely achievable in a home kitchen with commonly available ingredients. Serve it with warm naan bread or fragrant basmati rice to soak up every drop of that legendary sauce.

Ingredients

  • 800g boneless, skinless chicken thighs, cut into 5cm pieces
  • 200g plain full-fat yogurt (Greek-style, for the marinade)
  • 2 tbsp fresh lemon juice
  • 2 tsp Kashmiri red chile powder (or mild paprika plus a pinch of cayenne)
  • 1 tbsp garam masala (MDH or Everest brand recommended), plus 1 tsp for finishing
  • 1 tsp ground turmeric, 1 tsp ground cumin, 1 tsp ground coriander
  • 400g canned crushed tomatoes (Mutti or San Marzano)
  • 80g unsalted butter (divided: 50g for sauce, 30g for finishing)
  • 200ml heavy cream (35% fat minimum)
  • 1 tbsp dried fenugreek leaves (kasuri methi — this is the secret ingredient)
  • 4 cloves garlic, minced, and 2-inch piece fresh ginger, grated
  • 1 tbsp neutral oil (vegetable or sunflower) for searing chicken

Step-by-Step Instructions

  1. Marinate the chicken: In a large bowl, combine the yogurt, lemon juice, Kashmiri chile powder, half the garam masala, turmeric, cumin, coriander, 1 teaspoon salt, and half the minced garlic and ginger. Add the chicken pieces and toss until thoroughly coated. Cover and refrigerate for at least 30 minutes, or up to 8 hours for maximum flavor penetration. The yogurt tenderizes the chicken through gentle acidic action while the spices infuse deeply into the meat.
  2. Sear the chicken: Heat the oil and 1 tablespoon of butter in a large, heavy-bottomed skillet or Dutch oven over high heat until shimmering. Remove the chicken from the marinade, shaking off excess (reserve the marinade). Sear the chicken pieces in a single layer without moving them for 3-4 minutes until deeply golden brown on one side. Flip and sear for 2 more minutes. The chicken does not need to be cooked through — it will finish in the sauce. Transfer to a plate and set aside.
  3. Build the sauce base: Reduce heat to medium. In the same pan, add 50g of butter. Once it foams, add the remaining garlic and ginger and sauté for 60 seconds until fragrant and just beginning to turn golden. Add the crushed tomatoes, the remaining garam masala, and any reserved marinade. Stir well and bring to a simmer. Cook for 12-15 minutes, stirring occasionally, until the tomatoes have broken down completely, the oil begins to separate at the edges, and the sauce has reduced and deepened in color.
  4. Blend until silky: For the signature velvety texture, use an immersion blender to puree the sauce directly in the pan until completely smooth. Alternatively, carefully transfer to a standing blender (vent the lid to release steam) and blend until no chunks remain. Strain through a fine-mesh sieve back into the pan for absolute smoothness — this step is optional but authentic restaurant-style butter chicken always has a perfectly smooth sauce.
  5. Finish the sauce: Return the sauce to medium-low heat. Stir in the heavy cream and crush the dried fenugreek leaves between your palms as you add them to the sauce — this releases their aromatic oils and is truly the defining flavor of butter chicken. Add 1 teaspoon of sugar to balance the acidity of the tomatoes. Simmer gently for 5 minutes, stirring occasionally, until the sauce is rich, creamy, and coats the back of a spoon.
  6. Combine and simmer: Add the seared chicken pieces and any accumulated juices back into the sauce. Stir gently to coat every piece. Simmer on low heat for 8-10 minutes until the chicken is cooked through and tender, and the flavors have melded together. The sauce should be a beautiful warm orange color — rich but not heavy.
  7. Final touches: Remove from heat. Stir in the remaining 30g of butter and the final teaspoon of garam masala. The butter adds a final layer of richness and gloss to the sauce. Taste and adjust salt. The finished sauce should taste balanced — creamy, mildly spicy, slightly tangy, and warming from the spices without being hot.
  8. Serve: Transfer to a warm serving bowl. Drizzle with a swirl of cream, scatter a few whole dried fenugreek leaves on top, and add a sprinkle of fresh cilantro leaves. Serve immediately alongside steamed basmati rice, warm garlic naan, or both. A side of cooling cucumber raita and some pickled onions complement the dish beautifully.

Chef Tips

  • Kasuri methi is the secret: Dried fenugreek leaves (kasuri methi) are what distinguish great butter chicken from merely good butter chicken. They add a distinctive, slightly bitter, maple-like aroma that is unmistakable. Crush them between your palms before adding to release their essential oils. Available at any Indian grocery store — accept no substitutes.
  • Use chicken thighs, not breast: Thigh meat stays juicy and tender in the sauce, while breast meat tends to dry out and become chalky. The higher fat content of thighs also contributes to a more luscious overall dish. If you must use breast, reduce the simmering time by 3-4 minutes to prevent overcooking.
  • Kashmiri chile for color: Kashmiri chile powder is mild in heat but vibrant in color — it gives butter chicken its characteristic reddish-orange hue without making it fiery. If you cannot find it, use a combination of sweet paprika with just a small pinch of cayenne. Never use regular red chile powder, which will make the dish too spicy.
  • Do not rush the tomato cook-down: The tomatoes need a full 12-15 minutes of simmering to lose their raw, tinny flavor and develop a sweet, concentrated depth. You will know the sauce is ready when you see the oil separating at the edges — this is called "bhunao" in Indian cooking and is the sign that the masala is properly cooked.
  • Restaurant-style richness: For an even more indulgent, restaurant-quality result, stir in 2 tablespoons of cashew paste (cashews soaked and blended with a little water) along with the cream. This adds a subtle nutty richness and helps thicken the sauce to a silky, clinging consistency that coats each piece of chicken perfectly.
